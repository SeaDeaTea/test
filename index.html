The Basics

What is Dravet Syndrome?

Dravet Syndrome is a rare, severe form of epilepsy that begins in the first year of life — typically in an otherwise healthy infant. It was first described by Dr. Charlotte Dravet in 1978 and was known for years as "Severe Myoclonic Epilepsy of Infancy."

In most cases it's caused by a mutation in the SCN1A gene, which plays a critical role in how the brain regulates electrical signals. When this gene doesn't function correctly, the brain becomes prone to seizures that are difficult to control with standard medications.

Dravet is not degenerative — the brain does not worsen over time — but it is lifelong, and its effects on development, cognition, and behavior can be significant and vary widely from person to person.

🧬

Cause

A mutation in the SCN1A gene, usually occurring spontaneously — not typically inherited from parents.

📊

Prevalence

Affects approximately 1 in 15,700 people. Rare, but thousands of families are living with it right now.

🗓️

Onset

First seizures typically appear between 5 and 12 months of age, often triggered by fever or warm temperatures.

💊

Treatment

No cure exists, but seizures can be managed with medications, dietary therapy, and lifestyle adjustments.

What It Looks Like

Symptoms & Characteristics

Dravet presents differently from person to person, and even day to day. Seizures are the most visible symptom, but they're rarely the whole picture.

Seizure Types

  • Prolonged febrile convulsions (early on)
  • Tonic-clonic (grand mal) seizures
  • Myoclonic jerks — brief muscle twitches
  • Absence seizures — staring, unresponsive
  • Focal seizures affecting one side of the body
  • Status epilepticus — seizures lasting 30+ minutes

Developmental Impact

  • Delayed language and motor development
  • Cognitive and learning difficulties
  • Behavioral challenges (hyperactivity, impulsivity)
  • Autism spectrum traits in some individuals
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Balance and coordination issues (ataxia)

Common Seizure Triggers

🌡️ Fever or illness
☀️ Heat or hot environments
🏊 Warm baths or pools
💡 Flashing or flickering lights
😴 Overtiredness or poor sleep
⚡ Excitement or stress
🏃 Physical exertion
🌊 Visual patterns

For Bystanders

What to do during a seizure

If you're witnessing a seizure for the first time, it can be frightening. Most seizures pass on their own. Here's exactly what to do.

1
Stay calm and stay with them

Your presence matters. Don't leave them alone. Keep bystanders back to give space.

2
Clear the area of hazards

Move hard or sharp objects away. Cushion their head gently if they're on the ground.

3
Turn them onto their side

The recovery position keeps the airway clear. Guide gently — don't force it.

4
Note the time the seizure started

Duration is critical information for their family and any medical responders.

5
Contact their family

Reach the emergency contact listed on this page or their medical ID as soon as it's safe.

!
Call 911 if the seizure lasts more than 5 minutes

Also call if they stop breathing, don't regain consciousness, or have back-to-back seizures.

Do

  • Stay calm and stay present
  • Time the seizure from the start
  • Turn them gently onto their side
  • Cushion their head
  • Speak calmly — they may hear you
  • Stay until they are fully alert

Don't

  • Put anything in their mouth
  • Restrain their movements
  • Give food or water during the seizure
  • Leave them alone
  • Try to "wake them up" forcefully
  • Assume they're fine just because it stopped

Day to Day

What life with Dravet actually looks like

Dravet Syndrome is a 24/7 condition — but life doesn't stop. Families learn to adapt, plan ahead, and advocate fiercely. Here's a glimpse into what living with Dravet involves day to day.

🌡️

Temperature Management

Fever and heat are among the most reliable seizure triggers. Families monitor temperature constantly and act fast when illness strikes.

💊

Complex Medication Regimens

Most people with Dravet are on multiple seizure medications simultaneously, with careful monitoring for side effects and interactions.

🏫

School & Learning Support

Individualized education plans, aides, and adapted learning environments are often a necessary part of the picture.

🌙

Nighttime Monitoring

Seizures can occur during sleep. Many families use monitors, pulse oximeters, or stay in the same room for safety.

🏥

Specialist Care Team

Neurologists, developmental pediatricians, therapists, and dietitians may all be part of an ongoing care team.

❤️

Community & Advocacy

The Dravet community is small but fierce. Families advocate for research, awareness, and each other in powerful ways.
